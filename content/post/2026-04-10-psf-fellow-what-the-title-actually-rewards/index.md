I voted for 13 people this week.
Organizers. Translators. Teachers. A few developers too.
But the code — that's not why I voted yes.

I've been a Fellow of the Python Software Foundation since 2013. Every quarter, I read through the nominations. And every quarter, I run into the same problem.
Genuinely dedicated people, nominated for the wrong reasons.
"He maintains a library with 2 million downloads."
"She's been contributing to CPython for 8 years."
"His package is used by major companies."
That's real work. That's good work. But the Fellow distinction — that's not what it's for.

What I'm actually looking for when I read a nomination:
Did this person build something for others — not just a tool, but a community?
Did they organize events, pass on skills, make Python accessible where it wasn't before?
Does the Python community exist differently because of them?
It's not a technical prize. It's a way of saying: what you did for the community matters.

Let me give you a concrete example.
In 2022, I voted for Nicolas Laurance.
He had been organizing PyCon Ireland for years. Then Covid hit. The team went from around ten people down to two or three.
He kept going anyway.
Chasing sponsors. Booking speakers. Negotiating with the hotel. Designing t-shirts. Updating the website. Starting over the following year.
Months of work that nobody sees from the outside.
He was nominated by a mutual friend. I voted yes. He became a PSF Fellow.
That evening, I thought: the process worked exactly the way it was supposed to.

Here's what most people don't know: anyone can nominate someone.
You don't need to be a Fellow. You don't need to be on the board. One email is enough: psf-fellow@python.org
The nominations are open every quarter:
- **Q2 2026** — deadline: May 20th
- **Q3 2026** — deadline: August 20th
- **Q4 2026** — deadline: November 20th
More details: https://www.python.org/psf/fellows/

You probably know someone who deserves this.
Not necessarily the most visible person. Maybe the one who's been running a Python meetup in their city for six years. Or the person who translates documentation on weekends, without anyone really noticing.
These people exist. And usually, nobody thinks to nominate them.
Who comes to mind for you right now?
